Are you saving for retirement?

Typically, Any account that's non-taxable has a contribution limit.

  • Non-taxable (or tax-advantaged) accounts — like Roth IRAs, Traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, HSAs — usually have contribution limits set by the IRS.
  • Taxable accounts — like a regular brokerage account (or custodial) — do not have contribution limits. You can put in as much money as you want.

Tax-Advantaged Retirement Accounts

401(k)

  • Often provided by employers, and many offer matching contributions (free money!).
  • High contribution limits compared to IRAs.
  • Great for long-term retirement savings.
  • Contribution limit (2025): $23,000 for individuals under 50; $30,500 for those 50+ (catch-up).

Roth IRA

  • Tax-free withdrawals in retirement (after age 59½ and if the account is at least 5 years old).
  • Contributions can be withdrawn anytime, tax- and penalty-free.
  • Great for people who expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
  • Contribution limit (2025): $6,500 for individuals under 50; $7,500 for those 50+ (catch-up).
  • Income limits: Phased out for higher earners (single filers: $146,000 to $161,000; married couples: $218,000 to $228,000).

Traditional IRA

  • Contributions are tax-deductible, reducing taxable income in the year you contribute.
  • Great for people who need immediate tax savings.
  • Lower contribution limits than 401(k)s but still valuable for those looking to save outside of a workplace plan.
  • Contribution limit (2025): $6,500 for individuals under 50; $7,500 for those 50+ (catch-up).
  • Income limits: Deductions may be limited if you or your spouse are covered by a workplace plan.

HSA (Health Savings Account)

  • It's like a supercharged retirement account for health expenses.
  • Contributions are tax-deductible, earnings grow tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.
  • After age 65, you can use it for any purpose (though non-medical withdrawals are taxed).
  • Contribution limit (2025): $4,150 for individuals; $8,300 for families; $1,000 catch-up for those 55+.
  • Eligibility: Must have a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) to contribute.

Taxable Brokerage

A taxable Brokerage account is much more flexible, and can be opened with many firms. Unlike retirement accounts, there ar eno contribution limits, and no withdrawal restrictions.
However, you must pay capital gains taxes on profits and income taxes on dividents or interest earned. Its a great option for building wealth beyond retirement or saving for something like buying a house, starting a business, or early retirement.

If you're an active investor, consider looking in to margin accounts compared to cash accounts. To open a margin account within a taxable brokerage account, the minimum equity requirement is $2,000 in cash or marginable securities. This is mandated by FINRA and applies across all U.S. brokerages.

  • Initial Margin: Deposit at least 50% of the purchase price of securities. – FINRA
  • Maintenance Margin: Maintain at least 25% equity; brokers may require more. – FINRA
  • Pattern Day Trader: Maintain $25,000 equity if engaging in frequent day trading. – U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission

  • Cash account = Pay fully up front.
  • Margin account = Borrow to invest more, but it’s riskier and you’ll pay interest.

Note that if using a cash account, there is something called CB (cost basis). If you bought a share at $60, and sold for a loss at $40, and then bought that same security within the short-term, your original transaction would no longer be considered a "loss", and instead, your newest purchase would have that additional $20 difference applied to it. So now if you buy that same stock at $35, it will cost you $55. But don't be mistaken, this doesn't actually cost you anything extra, and is simply used as an anti tax fraud measure.

With cash accounts, you need to wait for funds to be settled and ready to trade with. the time it takes for these funds to settle from the market back into your account typically takes T + 2 days (Trade Date + 2 business days) to officially settle.

If you use those "unsettled" proceeds to buy another stock and then sell that new stock before the original funds settle, you trigger a Good Faith Violation. Your first or second violation will warrant a warning from your brokerage. However, 3+ violations within 12 months puts your account at risk of being restricted to "settled cash only" for 90 days (meaning you can only buy with fully settled funds), no instant re-trading.

Custodial accounts

There are a couple of main types of custodial accounts, typically used for managing assets for minors until they reach adulthood. Here are the most common types:

  • UGMA (Uniform Gifts to Minors Act) Accounts
    • Purpose: To hold and manage assets for a minor until they reach the age of majority (usually 18 or 21, depending on state law).
    • Types of Assets: Typically includes cash, stocks, bonds, and mutual funds.
    • Control: The custodian manages the assets until the minor comes of age, at which point the minor takes full control.
    • Taxation: The income generated from the investments is taxed at the minor's tax rate, which could be beneficial if the child is in a lower tax bracket.
  • UTMA (Uniform Transfers to Minors Act) Accounts
    • Purpose: Similar to the UGMA, but it allows for a broader range of assets, including real estate, artwork, and other types of property.
    • Types of Assets: Cash, stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and real estate (depending on state law).
    • Control: Like the UGMA, the custodian manages the assets until the minor reaches the age of majority, after which control is transferred to the minor.
    • Taxation: Like UGMA, the minor is taxed on any income generated by the assets.
  • Custodial Brokerage Accounts
    • Purpose: To invest in the stock market, mutual funds, ETFs, and other securities on behalf of a minor.
    • Types of Assets: Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, etc.
    • Control: The custodian manages the account until the minor reaches the age of majority.
    • Taxation: Investment income is subject to taxes, and for higher-income minors, the "kiddie tax" could apply, meaning some of the income might be taxed at the parents' tax rate.
  • Custodial Roth IRA
    • Purpose: To allow a minor to contribute to a Roth IRA, potentially building a tax-free retirement fund early on.
    • Types of Assets: Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, etc.
    • Control: The custodian manages the account, but the minor must have earned income to contribute to the Roth IRA (e.g., from a job or self-employment).
    • Taxation: Contributions are made with after-tax money, and qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

Key Differences:

  • UGMA vs. UTMA: The main difference is that UTMA accounts allow a broader range of assets, including real estate, while UGMA is limited to financial assets like stocks and bonds.
  • Custodial Roth IRA: This allows for tax-advantaged retirement savings and is beneficial if the minor has earned income.

When to Use a Custodial Account:

  • Savings for Education: Some parents use custodial accounts to save for a child’s education, though 529 plans are often preferred for educational savings due to their tax advantages.
  • Investing for the Future: These accounts can be used to teach children about investing and to give them a head start on building wealth.

Fidelity youth is another account for minors that exists, but isn't one of the officially named "custodial" accounts. The teen is the sole owner and decision-maker of the account, not a joint or custodial account. Most US stocks, ETFs, and mutual funds are tradable, and debit cards can be requested. Educational tools are also provided.

Education Savings Accounts:

Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)

  • Purpose: Save for K-12 and higher education expenses.
  • Tax Benefits: Contributions grow tax-deferred, and withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free.
  • Contribution Limits: $2,000 per year.
  • Eligibility: Income limits apply; account must be opened before the beneficiary turns 18.
  • Qualified Expenses: Tuition, books, supplies, tutoring, and special needs for K-12; tuition, books, and fees for college.
  • Control: The account holder manages the account until the beneficiary turns 30.

529 College Savings Plan

  • Purpose: Save for higher education expenses.
  • Tax Benefits: Contributions grow tax-deferred, and withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free.
  • Contribution Limits: Up to $300,000 or more per beneficiary.
  • Eligibility: No income limits; available for anyone.
  • Qualified Expenses: Tuition, fees, room and board, and books for college. Also covers up to $10,000 for K-12 tuition.
  • Control: The account owner retains control of the account and can change the beneficiary.

Trusts:

When are Trust Accounts Used?

  • Estate Planning: To transfer assets after death, avoiding probate.
  • Minimizing Taxes: To reduce estate taxes by shifting assets into a trust.
  • Asset Protection: To protect assets from creditors, lawsuits, or divorce.
  • For Minor or Special Needs Beneficiaries: To manage assets for children or those with special needs.
  • Charitable Giving: To donate assets to charity while getting tax benefits.
  • Control Over Distribution: To control how and when beneficiaries receive assets.
  • Avoiding Probate: To pass assets without going through the lengthy probate process.

Types of Trust Accounts

  • Revocable Trusts: Can be changed or revoked anytime.
  • Irrevocable Trusts: Cannot be changed after creation.
  • Living Trusts: Set up during the creator's lifetime.
  • Testamentary Trusts: Created by a will, active after death.
  • Special Needs Trusts: For beneficiaries with disabilities.
  • Charitable Trusts: Set up to donate to a charity.
