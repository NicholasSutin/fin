Otherwise, your next best bet is:

Scholarships

  • Reduce Debt Burden: Scholarships provide free money that doesn't need to be repaid, unlike loans. This can significantly reduce the amount of student debt you accrue, making it easier to manage your finances after graduation.
  • Lower Overall Costs: By securing a scholarship, you can cover part or all of your tuition, fees, or other expenses like textbooks, housing, and meals. This can lower the total cost of your education, making it more affordable in the long run.
  • More Financial Flexibility: With scholarships, you may be able to reduce your reliance on student loans or work-study jobs. This can allow you to focus more on academics or even participate in extracurricular activities or internships that could boost your career.
  • Better ROI: The cost of higher education can be significant. Scholarships improve the ROI of your degree by helping offset the cost. This can make your college education feel more like a worthwhile investment, especially if it helps you graduate with less debt.
  • Open Up Other Opportunities: Scholarships often come with additional benefits, like networking opportunities, mentorship, or job placements. These can improve your future career prospects and financial outcomes after you graduate.

Where to look

Start at your high school: Your school counselor can be a great resource for finding local scholarships. Many schools have scholarship listings and offer advice on how to apply for them.

Scholarship Search Websites:

  • Fastweb: One of the largest scholarship search engines where you can find scholarships based on your profile.
  • Scholarships.com: A comprehensive site where you can search for scholarships, grants, and other financial aid opportunities.
  • Appily: A site for college planning, scholarship searches, college reviews, and financial aid resources.
  • Niche: Helps students find scholarships, colleges, and reviews.

Many local businesses, community centers, and non-profits offer scholarships to high school students. Check with local libraries, religious institutions, and community foundations for opportunities. Many state governments offer scholarships or financial aid programs to in-state residents. Search your state's official website or the state department of education for scholarships available to local students.

Colleges and Universities:

Many colleges offer merit-based or need-based scholarships to incoming students. Check the financial aid section of the websites of colleges you're interested in. Some specific academic departments (like science, engineering, or humanities) offer scholarships for students who plan to major in those fields.

National Organizations, Corporations, Communities, and Associations:

  • The National Merit Scholarship Program: This is a prestigious scholarship based on PSAT scores and is available to high-achieving students.
  • STEM Scholarships: Organizations like the Society of Women Engineers (SWE) or the American Chemical Society offer scholarships for students pursuing science, technology, engineering, and math degrees.
  • Many large corporations offer scholarships to students pursuing higher education. For example, companies like Coca-Cola, Burger King, and McDonald's offer scholarships. Look at the corporate websites of companies you are interested in for scholarship programs.
  • Follow scholarship-related accounts on social media platforms like Instagram, Twitter, or Facebook. These accounts often share information about new scholarships and deadlines.
  • Reddit has various subreddits, such as r/scholarships, where users share information about scholarship opportunities.

Many scholarships are awarded through competitions or essay submissions. Look for opportunities to enter writing contests, art competitions, or other skill-based contests that offer scholarship money as a prize.
If you're considering a military career, check out the ROTC (Reserve Officers' Training Corps) programs or other military-related scholarships that offer financial aid in exchange for military service.

By starting your search early and applying to multiple scholarships, you can significantly reduce your financial burden when attending college.

internships:

  • Some internship programs, particularly those offered by large corporations, nonprofit organizations, or industry-specific groups, provide scholarships or financial awards to participants. These scholarships may be available for continuing education or to help fund college expenses.
  • Many government-sponsored internship programs, like those from NASA or the Department of Energy, offer scholarships to students pursuing specific fields of study.

Networking for scholarship leads:

  • Internships often put you in touch with professionals in your field of interest. These individuals might know of scholarship opportunities and can recommend specific programs or organizations offering financial aid.
  • Networking with mentors, co-workers, or other interns may help you gain insights into scholarship programs that you wouldn't have found on your own.

Strengthen Your Scholarship Applications:

  • Completing an internship allows you to build valuable skills and gain experience in your chosen field, which can be attractive to scholarship committees. Many scholarships are awarded based on academic achievement, extracurricular involvement, and work experience, all of which can be bolstered by an internship.
  • Some scholarships, particularly those aimed at future professionals (in fields like medicine, business, or engineering), may specifically look for internship experience as part of their criteria. Demonstrating hands-on experience in your field can set you apart from other applicants.
  • Some internships offer stipends or pay that can help offset some of your educational costs or living expenses. While not technically a scholarship, paid internships can provide financial relief, allowing you to focus more on your education.
  • Some universities have partnerships with companies or organizations that offer internships with scholarship components. If you're already applying to a particular college, inquire about any internship programs they might offer in collaboration with businesses or research centers that provide scholarships to students who participate.

Budgeting while in college:

  • Know your income: Include any financial aid, scholarships, part-time job income, parental support, or other sources of money.
  • Track your expenses: Break them down into categories (e.g., rent, food, transportation, entertainment) to get a clear picture of where your money is going. Apps like Mint or YNAB (You Need A Budget) can help automate this process.

Set a Realistic Budget

  • Set limits for each spending category based on your income and financial aid. Be realistic about your spending habits, and leave room for unexpected expenses.
  • Prioritize essentials like tuition, rent, utilities, and groceries. Cut back on non-essentials like eating out, entertainment, or shopping.
  • Limit Unnecessary Subscriptions. cancel the ones you don’t need. Even small monthly fees can add up over time. Consider sharing subscriptions with roommates or friends (e.g., Netflix, Spotify) to split the cost.
  • Eating out can be one of the biggest expenses for college students. Cooking at home is often much cheaper and can help you eat healthier. Plan your meals ahead of time, buy in bulk, and look for sales to save on groceries. Cooking large batches and freezing leftovers can also save you time and money.
  • Public transit is usually cheaper than owning a car or using ride-sharing services. If your campus is walkable, consider walking to get around.
  • Many colleges offer discounted or free transportation passes for students, so check if your school provides that.
  • Many campuses offer free resources like printing, tutoring, fitness centers, and events. Take advantage of these to avoid paying for services that are available to you for free. Look for free entertainment options such as campus movie nights, events, or outdoor activities organized by student groups.

Utilize Student Discounts:

  • Many businesses offer student discounts on everything from software and clothing to transportation and dining. Take advantage of these discounts whenever possible to save money.
  • Use student discount platforms like UNiDAYS and Student Beans to find deals on popular brands.

Build an emergency fund

  • Try to save a portion of your income for unexpected expenses like medical bills, car repairs, or sudden emergencies. Even a small emergency fund can prevent you from going into debt when something unplanned arises.

Avoid Credit Card Debt:

  • Credit cards can be a slippery slope if not managed carefully. Pay off balances in full each month to avoid high-interest charges. If you use a credit card, make sure you’re only charging what you can afford to pay back right away.
  • If you don’t have a credit card yet, try to build credit gradually, but avoid using it for everyday expenses unless you're confident you can pay off the balance immediately.

Useful tips:

  • Plan for major expenses like tuition, textbooks, and fees at the start of each semester
  • Set short-term and long-term financial goals to motivate you to stick to your budget
  • If your schedule allows, consider taking a part-time job or starting a side hustle to supplement your income. Many students find jobs in retail, food service, or as research assistants.
  • Life changes, and so should your budget. Review and adjust your budget regularly, especially if you get a new job, receive a scholarship, or have changes in your living situation.
  • Be sure you’re applying for all available financial aid options, including grants, scholarships, work-study programs, and loans. Don’t leave any free money on the table! Keep track of renewal dates for scholarships and financial aid so that you don’t miss out on funds for subsequent years.

By being proactive with your finances and following these budgeting tips, you can have a more financially stable college experience without constantly stressing about money.
